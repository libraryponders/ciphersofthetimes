THE BRIGHTON POISONING CASE.—JOHN
G, MAYNARD begs to tender his most grateful THANKS to
those ladies and gentlemen who have kindly not withhein their
patronage during the trying crisis through which he has lately passed,
and to assure them and the public generally that they may continue to
make their purchases at his establishment with undiminished confidonce.
The whole stock of French chocolate remaining at the time of the in¬
quest was immeniately afterwards destroyed, so that no portion of it
conid, by any means, be circulated among the public, and, in order to
prevent the possibility of any accident occurring in the future, he has
made a rule—to be stringently observed by his assistants that no
article of confectionery will, under any circumstances, be exchanged
after it has left the counter. The home-manufactured stock will, as
hitherto, be under the immediate personal superintendence of J. G. M.,
and warranted periectly pure, both as regards colour and composition,
and those goods which he has not facilities for making will be pur¬
chased from first class houses only, and with written guarantees from
the makers as to their freedom from everything in the slightest degree
injurious. By these means he hopes to restore the public confidence so
rudely snaken by recent events, and to regain the position he has held
during the last 28 years.—40, 41, and 42, West-street, Brighton, Sept.
11th, 1871.
